# Genre: Interview

The interview is a duet, and the typography must keep the two voices distinct at a glance.
The subject's presence — in portrait and in pulled speech — is the visual material; the
design's job is to make the reader hear them.

## Q&A typographic conventions

- Questions and answers are typographically distinct, consistently, for the whole piece.
  House convention: questions in **bold** (or Barlow Condensed medium), answers in regular
  text weight. Never label "Q:" / "A:" when weight contrast does the job.
- The interviewer's name appears once in the standfirst, not on every question.
- Long answers may take subheads pulled from their own content. Never break a single
  answer across a pull quote — the pull quote sits between Q&A units.
- An edited-down intro (150–300 words, set apart from the Q&A) establishes who this is
  and why now. It is the only place the writer's voice runs free.

## Portrait placement

- The opener portrait is the feature's dominant element: one strong portrait, generous
  scale — full-bleed if the image carries it, or large-on-white if it's a studio sitting.
- Subsequent spreads use smaller supporting portraits (gesture, environment, detail) as
  interruptions, roughly one per 2 spreads. Never repeat the same crop twice.
- The subject looks *into* the spread wherever possible — eyeline pointing off the page
  edge leaks the reader's attention with it.

## Pull quote as structural device

- In interviews the pull quote is structure, not decoration: it marks the emotional beats
  of the conversation. Pull the line a reader would text to a friend.
- Still one per spread maximum (universal rule) — but here, *use* that budget on most
  spreads; a quote-less interview spread is a missed beat.
- Pull only the subject's words, never the questions. Attribute with an em-dash and the
  subject's surname only when the piece has multiple voices; otherwise no attribution
  (the whole piece is theirs).

## Rhythm of density

- Vary Q&A density across spreads: an opener (portrait + intro), then a dense
  conversation spread (long answers, two columns), then an airy beat (short rapid-fire
  exchanges, or a portrait with a single pulled quote), then dense again.
- Rapid-fire sequences (one-line Q, one-line A) are a gift — set them as a distinct
  list-like block, tighter leading, and let them be the spread's fast passage.
- End on the subject's best closing line, set as the final pull quote or as the last
  answer with whitespace beneath. Never end on a question.

## Pattern preferences

Favor: `portrait-profile` (opener), `qa-alternating` (workhorse), `qa-rapid-fire`,
`quote-beat` (portrait + single pull quote), `quiet-single-column` (for the intro spread
of long interviews).
Avoid: `stat-punch`, `data-evidence`, `image-grid-quartet` (unless the subject's work is
itself visual — then borrow `artwork-plate` from art-culture with full credits).
