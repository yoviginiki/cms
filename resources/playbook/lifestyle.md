# Genre: Lifestyle

Warmth and usefulness. The reader is here for pleasure and takeaway in equal measure; the
design should feel like a well-set table — inviting, abundant, but composed. Photography
sets the temperature of the whole issue.

## Rhythm of many short items

- Lifestyle lives on multiplicity: a spread of five small discoveries beats one long essay
  about shopping. FOB energy extends deeper into the issue than in other genres.
- Each short item is a designed object: its own little headline, its own image or none,
  a firm boundary (rule or whitespace) from its neighbors. Items never blur together.
- Vary item scale on a spread — one anchor item at double size, satellites around it —
  so the eye has a route.

## Service journalism as designed objects

- Lists, how-tos, recipes, itineraries are **structures, not paragraphs**. Numbered steps
  set as steps; ingredients/requirements as a scannable column; the "if you go" facts in
  a box. The reader should be able to *use* the page.
- Every service piece answers: what do I do, what do I need, how long, how much. If the
  text doesn't say, the wizard asks the material for it — and if it's absent, the piece
  runs as a story, not a how-to.

## Photography

- Photography sets tone: natural light, real textures, human presence (hands, tables,
  streets) over sterile product shots. Warm over clinical.
- Full-bleed openers welcome for destination/food/interior features when the image has
  atmosphere. Detail shots (close crops of texture) work as small satellites around text.
- People are photographed mid-life, not posed: cooking, walking, working.

## Typography & warmth

- Same two faces as everywhere (Barlow Condensed / Barlow) — warmth comes from scale
  contrast, generous leading, and photography, not from script fonts.
- Italic is available for menus, place names, and asides — the genre's one softening
  device. Use it lightly.

## Pacing

- Fast–slow–fast: item-rich spreads, then one immersive photo feature, then items again.
- End the issue warm and small: a single image, a recipe, a reader's note — the design
  equivalent of a digestif.

## Pattern preferences

Favor: `fob-stack`, `item-mosaic`, `how-to-object`, `full-bleed-opener`,
`image-interruption`, `quiet-single-column` (for the one reflective essay), `closer-colophon`.
Avoid: `document-evidence`, `data-evidence` (a price-comparison table is fine; a bar
chart kills the mood), `text-well-three-column` (too newsy — two columns max).
